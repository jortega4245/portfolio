§ 02 · mathematical toolkit

Eight ideas I keep coming back to.

Not a skills list. These are the mathematical objects I reach for most, animated so you can see them work, with the formulas they live in. Everything below renders live in your browser.

∇ · optimization 01

Gradient descent on a loss surface

θt+1 = θt − η · ∇L(θt)

The workhorse under almost every modern model, from logistic regression to deep nets. A path through parameter space toward lower error, governed by step size η and the local shape of L.

ω · spectral 02

Discrete Fourier transform · periodogram

X(ωk) = Σt xt · e−i ωkt

Every signal is a sum of waves. The DFT decomposes a time series into frequency content, and the periodogram |X(ω)|² makes hidden cycles visible.

dWₜ · stochastic 03

Ornstein–Uhlenbeck mean reversion

dXt = θ(μ − Xt)dt + σ dWt

The continuous-time engine behind pairs trading. A drifting process pulled back toward its long-run mean. The mathematical reason cointegrated spreads are tradable in the first place.

∗ · convolution 04

Convolution · CNN feature maps

(f ∗ g)[i,j] = Σm,n f[m,n] g[i−m, j−n]

A small learned kernel sweeps across an input and reports what it sees. The basic operation under every image model, and the same math underneath time-series filters.

ℓ(θ) · likelihood 05

Maximum likelihood · iterative ascent

θ̂ = argmaxθ Σ log f(xi | θ)

When the score equation has a closed form, classical statistics hands you θ̂ in one line. When it doesn't, you climb the log-likelihood numerically. Same estimand, different machinery.

T*ᵦ · resampling 06

Bootstrap · empirical sampling distribution

T*b = T(X*b), b = 1 … B

When the analytic distribution of a statistic is hard, resample with replacement and let the data tell you. Confidence intervals, standard errors, and uncertainty without a textbook formula.

CLT · sampling distribution 07

Central Limit Theorem · the universal bell

√n (X̄n − μ) →d N(0, σ²)

Sample from anything with finite variance, take its mean enough times, and the histogram of those means goes Normal. The reason most statistical machinery built on Gaussian assumptions still works.

LLN · convergence 08

Law of Large Numbers · the long average

X̄n →a.s. μ as n → ∞

The running average of i.i.d. draws settles onto the true mean. The whole reason a long enough experiment, a long enough backtest, or a long enough survey starts to mean something.

§ 04 · classical vs computational

Two ways to find θ̂. Same target, different machinery.

A small case study I think about a lot. Maximum likelihood is one estimand with two personalities. Sometimes you get a closed form, sometimes you climb the log-likelihood numerically. The intuition is the same. The workflow is not.

classical

Score equation, solve directly

ℓ(θ; x) = Σᵢ log f(xᵢ ; θ)

∂ℓ/∂θ = 0   →   θ̂ in closed form

ex. Normal:  μ̂ = x̄,   σ̂² = (1/n)Σ(xᵢ − x̄)²

When the model is tractable (Gaussian, exponential, binomial) the score equation hands you the MLE algebraically. You also get the Fisher information for free, which gives asymptotic standard errors without simulation.

computational

Iterate toward the optimum

θ₀ ← initial guess

repeat:
   θ ← θ + α · ∇ℓ(θ)          // gradient ascent
   or  θ ← θ − H⁻¹∇ℓ(θ)       // Newton–Raphson

until ‖∇ℓ‖ < ε

When ∂ℓ/∂θ = 0 has no analytic solution (most realistic models, including anything with constraints, latent variables, or nonlinearities) you climb the log-likelihood instead. The bootstrap then steps in for the standard errors Fisher used to give you.
